NSF Data Sharing Policy Overview

Required since 2011 — reinforced and expanded through PAPPG updates in 2025 and 2026

The National Science Foundation has required Data Management Plans since 2011. The policy is grounded in NSF's expectation that investigators share research products with the broader scientific community.

📋

Core expectation (PAPPG XI.D.4): NSF-funded investigators are expected to share with other researchers, at no more than incremental cost and within a reasonable time, the primary data, samples, physical collections, software, inventions, curriculum materials, and other supporting materials created or gathered in the course of work under NSF awards.

📄 The DMP Document

  • A supplementary document of no more than two pages labeled "Data Management and Sharing Plan"
  • Submitted in the supplementary documents section — not counted against the project description page limit
  • Proposals without a DMP cannot be submitted and will be returned without review
  • Even if the project generates no data, a justification document must replace the DMP

📅 Key Policy Milestones

  • 2011: DMPs first required for all NSF proposals
  • 2016: Public access requirement added — publications in NSF-PAR
  • 2023: DMPs subject to peer review as part of merit review
  • Dec 8, 2025 (Suppl. 1): Embargo removed; publications freely available at time of publication
  • Jan 22, 2026 (Suppl. 2): Data sharing at time of publication required; Research.gov tool announced
  • Apr 27, 2026: New Research.gov DMP tool launches — becomes required submission method
⚡

Key difference from NIH: NSF DMPs are evaluated by peer reviewers as part of merit review, and past DMP performance is evaluated in subsequent proposals under "Results from Prior NSF Support." Generic, boilerplate plans receive poor scores.

Who Must Include a DMP?

Nearly all NSF proposals — with very limited exceptions
✅

The short answer: All full research proposals submitted to NSF must include a DMP. This applies regardless of award size, type of data generated, or directorate. There is no funding threshold equivalent to NIH's old $500K rule.

✅ Must Include a DMP

  • All standard research grants (including collaborative proposals)
  • CAREER awards and other early-career mechanisms
  • Major Research Instrumentation (MRI) proposals
  • RAPID, EAGER, and RAISE proposals
  • REU supplements and international research proposals
  • Proposals generating no data (submit a justification instead)

⚠️ Special Situations

  • No data generated: Submit a brief justification (e.g., purely theoretical work, workshop with no report) — a placeholder is still required
  • Sensitive data: Privacy, confidentiality, security, IP, or legal restrictions are acceptable — but must be documented and justified in the DMP
  • Industry-collaborative data: Proprietary restrictions must be explained in the DMP at time of original submission
  • Program solicitations: Always check the FOA — some programs override or supplement PAPPG defaults
💡

Unlike NIH, NSF does not have an exemption list of activity codes. If you submit to NSF, you need a DMP. The only question is what your specific directorate or program requires beyond the PAPPG minimum.

What Counts as "Data" Under NSF?

NSF's scope is broader than NIH's — explicitly includes samples, software, and curriculum materials
📖

NSF definition: "Data are defined as the recorded factual material commonly accepted in the scientific community as necessary to validate research findings. This includes original data, but also 'metadata' (e.g., experimental protocols, code written for statistical analyses, etc.)."

📊 Primary Data

Experimental results, observational data, survey responses, interview transcripts, field measurements, sensor data, images, audio/video recordings

🧪 Samples & Collections

Physical samples, specimens, biological materials, geological cores, archaeological artifacts, and physical collections created or gathered under the award

💻 Software & Code

Source code, scripts, algorithms, computational models, and workflows developed as part of the research — especially if needed to reproduce findings

📐 Curriculum Materials

Educational content, teaching modules, and workshop materials developed with NSF funding — particularly relevant for EDU and EHR directorate proposals

📝 Metadata & Documentation

Digital lab notebooks, experimental protocols, data dictionaries, codebooks, and README files needed to interpret and reproduce the research

📄 Publications & Outputs

Author Accepted Manuscripts deposited in NSF-PAR upon acceptance with no embargo (as of Dec 8, 2025); datasets supporting publications shared at time of publication (as of Jan 22, 2026)

💡

NSF vs. NIH: NIH explicitly excludes preliminary analyses, drafts, and lab notebooks from its definition of "scientific data." NSF's scope is broader — digital lab notebooks and metadata are considered part of your data products and should be addressed in your DMP.

The Five Required DMP Elements

PAPPG II.D.2.i.ii — general guidelines applicable to all NSF proposals

Every NSF DMP must address these five categories of information. Directorate-specific requirements add detail — but all five must be present in some form in every DMP.

1
Types of Research Products
Describe the types of data, samples, physical collections, software, curriculum materials, and other materials to be produced. Include estimated formats, volumes, and any specialized conditions for collection or storage.
2
Standards for Data & Metadata
Identify the standards to be used for data and metadata format and content. If no adequate community standard exists, document this and describe your proposed documentation approach or remedy.
3
Policies for Access & Sharing
Describe policies for data access and sharing, including protections for privacy, confidentiality, security, intellectual property, or other rights. Address any restrictions and justify them. Per Supplement 2, datasets supporting publications must be shared at time of publication.
4
Policies for Reuse & Redistribution
Describe policies for reuse, redistribution, and the production of derivatives. Address licensing (CC0, CC-BY, MIT License for software), attribution requirements, and any restrictions on how others may use or build on the data.
5
Plans for Archiving & Preservation
Describe plans for archiving data, samples, and other research products and for preserving access to them. Identify a specific repository, address long-term preservation commitments, and explain how access will be maintained after the award ends.
📌

Important: These five elements are the minimum. Your directorate, division, or program solicitation may require significantly more specificity. DMPs that are generic and do not address project-specific data will receive poor peer review scores.

2026 Policy Updates: PAPPG Supplement 2

Effective January 22, 2026 — major changes to publication and data sharing timelines
⚡

PAPPG 24-1 Supplement 2 was released January 22, 2026 and applies to all financial assistance awarded on or after that date. It implements NSF's "Gold Standard Science" public access plan and significantly tightens data and publication sharing requirements.

📰 Publications (AAMs)

  • Author Accepted Manuscripts must be deposited in NSF-PAR upon acceptance
  • No embargo — publications freely available at time of publication (Supplement 1, Dec 8, 2025)
  • Machine-readable XML metadata required in NSF-PAR
  • Persistent identifiers (DOIs, NSF-PAR IDs) must appear in project reports

💾 Datasets

  • Datasets supporting NSF-funded publications must be shared at time of publication — no longer "within a reasonable time"
  • Data must be deposited in repositories that assign persistent identifiers (DOIs)
  • Direct dataset entry in Research.gov project reports is prohibited — use repository DOIs instead
  • Any exceptions must be justified in the DMSP at time of original proposal submission
🛠️

Research.gov DMP Tool — Launching April 27, 2026: NSF will release a new online tool within Research.gov for creating and submitting DMSPs. Until then, proposals must continue to upload the DMP as a 2-page PDF. Once the tool launches, it becomes the required submission method.

📌

What to include in your DMP now: Explicitly state that datasets supporting publications will be deposited in a DOI-issuing repository and made available at time of publication. Any exceptions — proprietary data, privacy constraints, IP restrictions — must be described and justified upfront.

FAIR Data Principles

NSF encourages FAIR practices — especially critical as peer reviewers evaluate your DMP

While NSF does not require FAIR compliance by name (unlike NIH), FAIR principles align directly with what peer reviewers look for. A plan that is findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable will score well in review.

FFindable
  • Assign a DOI or accession number via a repository
  • Use rich, descriptive metadata
  • Register data in an indexed, searchable catalog
AAccessible
  • Data retrievable via open standard protocols
  • Metadata accessible even if data is restricted
  • Clear authentication/authorization process
IInteroperable
  • Use community-standard file formats
  • Include controlled vocabularies in metadata
  • Enable integration with other datasets
RReusable
  • Clear license (CC0, CC-BY, MIT for code)
  • Detailed provenance documentation
  • Meets community standards for your field
💡

Peer reviewers ask: Can another researcher find this data? Access it? Understand and use it? Build on it? Framing your DMP around FAIR principles — even without using that language — will strengthen your proposal and demonstrate methodological rigor.

NSF does not endorse specific repositories — but peer reviewers expect a well-justified, domain-appropriate choice

Human Subjects & Sensitive Data

DMP Element 3 — access and sharing policies must address privacy and confidentiality protections

If your research involves human subjects, your DMP must explicitly address how you will protect participant privacy, rights, and confidentiality while still fulfilling NSF's data sharing expectations.

📋 Consent Language

Consent forms should anticipate future data sharing. Include language indicating that de-identified data may be deposited in a public or controlled-access repository. IRBs increasingly expect this language in consent documents for NSF-funded research.

For existing data, document whether prior consent allows secondary use and sharing — and if not, explain why sharing is limited.

🔒 De-identification & Controlled Access

Data shared publicly must be de-identified following recognized standards (HIPAA Safe Harbor, expert determination). Controlled-access repositories (ICPSR restricted collections, NACDA, secure enclaves) are appropriate for data that cannot be fully de-identified.

📚 FERPA and Student Data

Education research involving student records is subject to FERPA. NSF EDU proposals must address FERPA compliance. De-identified aggregate data is generally shareable; individually identifiable records require strong access controls.

🏛️ Indigenous & Tribal Data Sovereignty

NSF requires consultation with Tribal Nations for proposals impacting Tribal resources. Data sovereignty principles apply — communities may have rights over data collected from or about their members. Address this explicitly in your DMP if applicable.

⚠️

Justifying restrictions: Privacy, security, IP, or legal constraints are acceptable reasons to limit sharing — but they must be detailed in the DMP at original proposal submission (PAPPG Supplement 2). "We haven't decided yet" is not acceptable. NSF expects a clear data sharing strategy before submission.

Peer Review & Compliance

NSF DMPs are peer reviewed — and prior DMP performance affects future proposals
📝
Submit
2-page PDF in supplementary docs. No DMP = returned without review.
🔍
Peer Review
Reviewers evaluate DMP as part of merit review. Generic plans hurt scores.
✅
Award
DMP becomes part of award terms. Implement as proposed.
⚙️
Implement
Deposit data, share publications in NSF-PAR, maintain documentation.
📊
Report
Annual/final reports document compliance. NSF-PAR IDs auto-populate.
📋

Prior NSF support: Subsequent proposals must describe DMP outcomes under "Results from Prior NSF Support." Poor compliance on past awards is visible to reviewers and can affect funding decisions.

⚠️

Non-compliance consequences: NSF may impose special conditions on awards, restrict future funding, or take other enforcement action. NSB and Committees of Visitors monitor DMP compliance agency-wide.

💡

Modifying your DMP: Contact your NSF program officer if data management plans change significantly. Changes constituting a change in objectives or scope require NSF approval per PAPPG Chapter VII.B.1.

Sample DMP Walk-Through

Example: NSF SBE grant — longitudinal survey study of civic engagement with survey data and interviews
📌

This example addresses all five PAPPG elements plus SBE-specific human subjects requirements. Notice how each section is specific to the project — not generic boilerplate. This is what reviewers want to see.

1
Types of Products
Project will generate: (a) longitudinal survey data from 1,200 participants across 3 waves (SPSS and CSV formats, ~2GB); (b) semi-structured interview transcripts from 60 participants (de-identified Word/PDF, ~500MB); (c) Python and R analysis scripts. No physical samples or curriculum materials.
2
Standards
Survey data will use DDI (Data Documentation Initiative) metadata standards, consistent with ICPSR deposit requirements. Interview transcripts accompanied by project-specific codebooks. Python/R code documented with inline comments and a README following community conventions. No consensus standard exists for qualitative transcripts; project documentation will follow QUAGOL guidelines.
3
Access & Sharing
De-identified survey data deposited in ICPSR as open access upon publication. Interview transcripts deposited in ICPSR restricted collection — access granted to researchers upon data use agreement approval. Analysis code shared publicly on GitHub with a Zenodo DOI at time of publication. All data available no later than time of associated publication per PAPPG Supplement 2.
4
Reuse & Redistribution
Survey data: CC0 (public domain). Interview transcripts: restricted reuse per ICPSR data use agreement; redistribution not permitted without PI approval. Code: MIT License — freely usable, modifiable, and redistributable with attribution. All products may be used for secondary analysis, replication, and teaching.
5
Archiving & Preservation
ICPSR provides 25-year minimum preservation commitment with DataCite DOIs. Zenodo provides CERN-maintained long-term preservation for code. Local copies maintained on Tulane institutional storage during award. All data available no later than the project end date.
👥

Human subjects note: Consent forms include IRB-approved language permitting de-identified data sharing in a public repository and restricted sharing of interview transcripts under controlled access. Data de-identified per HIPAA Safe Harbor prior to deposit. IRB approval [date] covers all data management and sharing activities described.
